Context

ABOUT ANZA TRAILS & THE PROJECT
My employer wanted to propose their client a mobile app for people that wanted access info on the go since the current website isn't responsive. My employer was GreenInfo Network, non profit. Their client was the Anza Historic Trails - National Park Service. The Anza territory is the route used by the colonizer Juan Bautista De Anza.

Challenges
•Lack of contact with the client from the beginning
•Tensions between employer and client needs including low budget.
•Multiple institutions collaborating with inaccurate trail data.

Results
GreenInfo Network proceeded to use my research and recommendations for other projects since the NPS had limited budget that couldn't cover GIN's fee.

Timeline
4 weeks part time - October/November 2014

Role - Team
UX Researcher and Designer - Solo

This project is related to:
UX Mobile App Responsive Website Hiking Trails History Travel Geospatial Data Maps Information Design Information Architecture Content Strategy Interaction Design Human-Computer Interaction

Problems found
Big part of the Anza territory has bad mobile coverage. Planning ahead is a must. But map data is inaccurate because there are many institutions giving data. Many needed details cannot be provided, but people don't expect that. What can be provided is info hard to find and hard to understand (use of technical language).

Proposal
To make the website for mobiles. It manages people’s expectations of how accurate the information can be. It provides links to info missing and makes info available visible. That required translating technical terms into common language. Thus, I gave directions to change map collector language and display.

The process wasn't linear. At the beginning I didn't have access to the client. So I did the app and afterwards I met the client by accident, while user testing with a ranger.

I learnt why the data was inaccurate among other things. They wouldn't invest in an app, but into making the website good for mobile use.

1. Research

Content Inventory
I made sure to understand what the anzahistorictrails.org contained before interviewing the users. In this case, there was a particular part of the website relevant to "on the go" experiences: the map. It had 3 tabs: one for amenities, one for directions, and one for historic journals. What changes is the layer key, but it's not noticeable.

Competitive Analysis
I researched the state of the art of trail apps. Either they tracked the user's path or they gave information of the activities allowed. I found only few of them that provided an acceptable to good experience. After interviewing users, I noticed only one of those apps actually addressed people's needs.

Go to the website here. This image is a screenshot of the map page. Notice where it says "Map Correction or Addition?". That is where you will find the form to collect data of the trails.


The only trail app I found that addressed people's needs: M app


Another example that was more simple and worked for Boulder County


I organized the interviews data into "what" activities people performed, "how" they did it and "why". I could identify what type of information they seek out of Anza and the pain points of the experience: from planning a trip on the desktop to mobile access in the trail


Interview discoveries
•The Anza historic trail is a territory that contains many parks and jurisdictions. They all contributed to the trail data provided in the website, hence most of it is a mess. The NPS has a single employee cleaning it up.

•People first need information to plan their trips Still, people forget details on the go and visit the website for help, but it isn't helpful for 2 reasons:

-The website isn't responsive and it's hard to access from a mobile. Plus, most of the territory has bad mobile signal.

-People only use the map, but the map has inaccurate and missing data. People don't expect this.

• People want: first hand history sources, high level info of community events, and nature and physical attributes of the trails.
Funny thing is that the website actually has that information. But it is organized in a way that fails to show it.

• Hikers need more detailed info on each trail and site, but Anza cannot provide it.

• Historians need to make a difference between centers, museums, or sources of information. NPS can't provide that either.

• My conclusion: NPS can only ask better questions/info to their collaborators. The form to add data needs to change.


This image shows the info people ask for and need is there, but you can barely see a scramble of technical words that make no sense. This should be "translated" and displayed graphically.

Personas
I divided and grouped the data across several variables: type of info they seek and how they assimilate it, goals and behaviors. Then, I gave each group the personality traits of the correspondent people interviewed. The main persona is Carlos because he is the most recurrent website user. John would be the main user of the trail data collection form.

2. Ideation

Scenarios, User Flows, MVP and Sitemap
1. I used scenarios to imagine what Carlos, Henry and John might do and feel. This helped me create micro-user flows as solutions to each scenario. Thus, I also determined features needed.
2. I prioritized actions and features according to the personas' goals.
3. I designed the sitemap and the user flow according to that prioritization.

This only work if I have absolute understanding of the users, which is why I prefer to be the interviewer in all my projects.

Interface sketching and design definition
Sketching each main screen helped me see the holes in the user flow and helped me make adjustments. I asked friends and colleagues for quick feedback then I began digitizing approved ideas in Illustrator.

3. Prototyping

Wireframes, Prototype and User Testing Round 1
I finished the app wireframes and used InVision to prototype it. I reached out to the same people I had interviewed. I received positive feedback from the users. However, I met the client by accident. Interviewing the client was clarifying, but it changed my plan.

Wireframes, Prototype and User Testing Round 2
I had to review the content of the website and leave out any feature that didn't exist already. I tried the mobile website approach prototype and received positive feedback. Anza supported my idea of changing the collaborator but could only pay so much, so the decision relied on GreenInfo.

4. Presentation

Presentation to GreenInfo Network
My employer felt satisfied with my work. They felt impressed with how I dealt with the client and tried to solve their concerns. They decided to implement by suggestions and bargain with the NPS for funding.

Learnings
My major learning with this project was that I have to ask and insist for the things I need to work properly like having direct access to the client.
